The first thing most new sidemount divers get wrong is the bungee. Not the clip positions. Not the D-ring heights. The bungee.

Specifically: the tension. And more specifically: whether it's doing what you think it's doing.

## What the bungee is actually for

The bungee in sidemount is a retaining mechanism — it holds the cylinder butt against the body to stop it swinging forward and disrupting your trim. It is **not** a suspension system. It is not there to carry the cylinder's weight.

If your bungee is under tension at the surface with no cylinder attached, something has gone wrong before you even picked up a tank.

The bungee should have just enough tension to sit flush against the cylinder butt when the cylinder is correctly positioned at the hip. No slack, no yank.

## The test

Here's the test I use with every student before we ever enter the water:

1. Clip up the cylinder in your standard position
2. Slide the bungee over the cylinder butt
3. Run your thumb under the bungee at the midpoint

You should be able to slide your thumb under with mild resistance — like a watch strap that fits correctly. If you can fit two fingers, the bungee is too slack. If you can barely fit one fingertip, it's too tight.

That single test resolves the majority of trim problems I see in the first five minutes of a skills session.

## Why tension matters

Too loose: the cylinder butt lifts off the hip on descent as the wetsuit compresses. The front of the cylinder (valve end) stays clipped but the butt floats out. You lose your horizontal profile. You're now fighting physics on every dive.

Too tight: the cylinder is pinched inward. On ascent, as the wetsuit expands, there's no room. The bungee either pops off or puts asymmetric load on the hip clip. Either way, something unexpected happens at a moment when you have other things to think about.

## The depth variable

This is where it gets more interesting. Neoprene compresses with depth. A bungee that feels correct at the surface may be too slack at 40 metres. For divers working in thick wetsuits (7mm+) or drysuits with significant undersuit bulk, this is worth factoring into your setup.

The standard approach: test at the surface, note how the cylinder feels in the first few metres of descent, and adjust bungee length between dives until it's consistent across the depth range you're working in.

Some divers use a second bungee loop for deep technical work. I don't — I find a single correctly-tensioned bungee more reliable than two that can twist against each other — but your mileage may vary.

## What material matters

Not all bungee is equal. Cheap bungee stretches permanently after a few dozen dives and loses its tension completely. The cylinder then migrates outward, your trim suffers, and you spend the next three dives wondering what's changed.

Use shock cord rated for marine use, or bungee specifically sold for dive equipment. Replace it when you notice it's lost its snap. It's inexpensive enough that there's no argument for running old bungee.

## Bottom line

Before you change your cylinder positions, your D-ring heights, your trim weights, or your clip style — check the bungee. In my experience, it solves the problem more than half the time.

The correct setup feels almost boring. The cylinder doesn't move. It doesn't swing forward on descent. It doesn't peel away from the body on ascent. It just sits there, exactly where you put it, doing its job. That's the goal.
